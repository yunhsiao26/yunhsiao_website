Microsoft Academic

Providing insights on research trends.

Project Type:

Design Challenge

Duration:

Aug. 2019 (6 days) + Sept. 2019 (1 day)

Practice Areas:

UX design, information architecture, interaction design, information visualization, visual design, design pitch

My Role:

I took ownership of this individual project.

Project Vision

Microsoft Academic is a knowledge graph which supports academic influence evaluation with a free public web search engine for academic publications. The front-end website also serves as a place to present and promote the knowledge graph. Among six entities on the website, "conference" was the main focus in this design challenge. I redesigned the conference entity detail page (taking KDD as an example) by creating reasonable information architecture, providing a "multi-sense” similar conference feature, and enriching the web page with multiple information visualizations to support an intuitive and engaging user experience.

Challenges

Design a reasonable layout with limited understandings of user needs and behaviors.

Create legible, neutral, and engaging information visualizations.

Prevent information overload while providing multiple visualizations.

Design Process

User Need Assumptions

The project goal was to fix current design issues, and design a “multi-sense” similar conference feature and multiple data visualizations to support an efficient conceptualization of the trend of a particular field of study. In the beginning, I explored the website focusing on KDD entity detail page (EDP) and conference entity type analytics page (ETAP). With limited time and resources, I chose to skip user studies but made some assumptions about user needs and behaviors to support the design work. These assumptions could be tested and verified in the future stage:

Users cared about the influence of an entity (venue, author, institution, etc.).

Users would like to know the top influential entities and might want to explore more.

Users cared about venues more than authors and institutions on EDP.

Design Decisions

Based on the above assumptions, I made three key design decisions to support user needs:

Adding H-index in the basic information section for user reference, and creating hyperlink linking to Conference ETAP.

Following the current website user flow: Conference Basic Info —> Venue Publication & Citation Visualization —> Top Author Visualization —> Institution Visualization.

Presenting top entities (taking 10 as an example) in visualizations.

Ideation

With the decisions in mind, my next step was brainstorming potential design solutions by sketching down ideas. Without a design guideline to follow, I also figured out overall visual style, theme colors, typeface, font size, etc. before starting hi-fi design work.

Overall, I tried to engage users and create an energetic, modern, and up-to-date impression by designing an interface with a cleaner, brighter, and softer style. Blue was chosen as theme color to align with the logo mark and add a trustworthy, authoritative vibe. While picking secondary and tertiary colors, I followed the WCAG AA standard and avoided using complementary colors with the same values in the same diagrams to take care of legibility, color blindness, and color harmony.

Besides, typefaces with legibility and more neutral style were used – Open Sans and Helvetica Neue. To create a clear visual hierarchy and enhance readability, I set three headings and used 16px (12pt) as the base font size for paragraphs.

Design Iteration

In the next stage, I drew hi-fi wireframes for KDD EDP to present how my ideas could work. I iterated the design for four rounds to support smoother user experience. Below are four key changes I made along the way:

I redesigned the layout by placing basic info closely, horizontally in the first version, and fixed the filter part to make it align with the right-side visualizations. However, the header would be cut into two while page scrolling down. Therefore, I regrouped the elements on the left side and fixed both basic info and the filter section to avoid conflicts. I also replaced infinite scroll with tabs for visualizations to shorten the webpage and reduce information overload. In order to keep a consistent style, I later used a card to group basic info and moved the action buttons to an obvious place.

The interaction for visualizations was improved. I designed drop-down menus to support the selection of a desired period of time, and integrated two drop-down menus to reduce the number of clicks. Users could select a start year and an end year right on the same drop-down menu. Besides, I also added vertical guidance behind visualizations to help users quickly catch the corresponding values on the x-axis.

I refined the “multi-sense” similar conference feature to make it more engaging. In the beginning, it was designed as recommendation lists placing at the bottom of the page. I later took mobile usage into consideration and made them visually more comfortable. However, the feature still looked plain. I thought utilizing visualizations may enhance engagement, so three visualizations for co-citation, co-authorship, and co-concept top 3 venues were generated. The order could be decided with the importance/popularity of the information. I also refined the titles for each section to reach precision and conciseness. Lastly, I integrated them for users to interact with more conveniently.

I changed the labels for the “Top Authors in Microsoft Academic Rank” visualization. The original labels for the x-axis and y-axis are “MA Rank” and “MA Rank Normalized by Publications.” However, users may not familiar with the concept of “normalization.” Therefore, I replaced them with “Influence Ranking” and “Average Influence Ranking,” trying to make the concept easier to grasp.

Final Design

Through four rounds of iteration, the final design is presented below. In KDD EDP new design, the top part was a fixed global navigation bar with Microsoft Academic logo, search bar, citation list, and sign up / sign in button. Below, users could see the name of the conference and basic into at the first sight, accompanying with key dates, conference proceedings and action buttons. On the bottom-left side was the filter section where users could narrow down topics for visualizations. The default view for the visualization section was "Citation". Users could shift between “Author,” “Institution,” and “Related” venues through the tabs.

There were three visualizations on "Citation" tab to present the influence and trend of this conference. First, a line chart was used to replace the original bar chart for publications and citations over time to better present the variation. Second, both incoming and outgoing citations were integrated on the same visualization, with ideas borrowed from Sankey diagram to present both flow amount and direction at the same time. Third, citation evolution could be seen through a comparison of both time points and durations. Top five were set as default (based on the most recent time period selected), but users could hide them or search and add more. A maximum amount may need to be set with a warning message when the limitation is reached.

On the author tab, there were two visualizations to help users quickly know who are the most important/influential authors of a conference in a particular period of time. First, a bubble chart combining with a two-dimensional space was used to present three-dimensional datasets for top authors in Microsoft Academic Rank (MA Rank). Exact numbers of publications and citations were presented on hover pop-up for user reference. Besides, an info icon was placed beside the title with a small hover pop-up on which a brief explanation would be provided to explain “MA Rank” for users. The second one was a line chart for the citation evolution of authors. It was designed based on the same principle for the citation evolution visualization.

For institutions, there were two visualizations to present which institutions have the most influence on KDD as well as their locations. First, a bubble chart was used for top-cited institutions. Top ten was presented to help users easily focus on the top entities and reduce information overload, but more could be added on if needed. The second one was a world map for the headquarters of institutions, with addresses shown on hover pop-up. Here, a closer view was set as default.

The last tab was for related conferences and journals, serving more like a recommendation feature. Here, the values of the similarity with co-citation, co-authorship, and co-concept top 3 venues were presented with dots in one visualization for users to explore.

Reflection

Limitation

User studies could be conducted to collect user preferences, to validate assumptions, and to know more about edge cases.

If given more time, a competitive analysis could be conducted to borrow good ideas and avoid mistakes made.

Next Step

Evaluate the design through usability testing and interview to collect feedback from target users, and iterate the design based on research insights.

Bring in engineers and other stakeholders of the project to ensure the design will eventually reach a balance among usability, feasibility, and viability.

Define the MVP and focus on the most important features in the beginning.

Create mobile-friendly design if needed.

What I Learned

Consistency across visualizations is what designers could take care of while designing multiple / a series of visualizations for a platform.

In addition to demonstrating and promoting the knowledge graph, helping both graduate students and established researchers quickly understand a new area of interest / field of study, including getting a map of the trends, main research areas, most important papers and authors, etc. is what Microsoft Academic also aims to serve. (I comprehended this point after joining the team.)
