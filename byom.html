B.Y.O.M.

Promoting environmental protection.

Project Type:

CHI Design Competition

Duration:

Sep. – Dec. 2017

Team Size:

5 members

Practice Areas:

User studies, UX design, interaction design, information visualization, visual design, usability evaluation

My Role:

I coordinated the team, led the ideation sessions, and took charge of the hi-fi design of the app.

Project Vision

B.Y.O.M. (Bring Your Own Mugs) is a service combining a smart sleeve for water bottles and a mobile app to support reducing disposable cup usage and forming eco-friendly community. There were 80 billion disposable cups used in the U.S. each year, and almost all disposable cups were made of non-recyclable materials. There was no efficient solution broadly adopted either. Therefore, we aimed to design a service that could motivate students bring their own mugs to free food events. We were looking forward to seeing the community to be broadened when users engage other people in their lives and thus greater waste reduction can be achieved.

Challenges

Tackle individual level issues such as forgetfulness, eco-friendly awareness, and social norms.

Motivate students to change their existing habits of using disposable cups and participate in long-term eco-friendly behaviors by engaging them in this service.

Enlarge eco-friendly communities by arousing eco-awareness and utilizing interpersonal relationship and social networks.

Design Process

User Studies

In order to know more details about people’s behaviors and motivations in using their own mugs and disposable cups, we conducted the first-round primary research with accessible resources. We went to an on-campus free food event and observed and interviewed 36 students. Only 1/5 of attendees brought their own reusable tumblers, and there were 4 main motivations of bringing one’s own mugs, and 4 main negative factors influencing user behaviors:

Why?

Habit

“It has become a natural habit just like bringing keys or wallets.”

Eco-friendly Awareness

“My high school teacher made me aware of what I use in my daily life and what impact I might have on the environment.”

Convenience

“I want to keep the drink hot longer.”

Social Norms

“I think I’m influenced by my parents and friends. I’m not the first person in my family to start this behavior.”

Why Not?

Lack of Suitable Mugs

“I recently lost my mug. I don’t have a good one to bring with me now.”

Forgetfulness

“I always forgot to bring my own thumbler. I need to be more organized about it.”

Already Filled with Water

“I have my water bottle with me and I always use water refill stations at school. I’ve never used it for beverage.”

Social Norms

“My friends and family don’t bring one with them either. This idea never occur to me.”

Analysis & Ideation

Based on the above studies, we framed our challenge into "How to motivate people to change their existing habits of using disposable cups and use their own mugs at free food events?" We also created corresponding personas for the three types of eco-friendly awareness categorized. Considering about people’s behaviors, we believed that we could make the most impact with students having medium-level eco-friendly awareness by providing services that can increase their motivation to use their own mugs and even to influence people around them. Therefore, we decided to take this group of students as our target users.

We later held two brainstorming sessions focusing on 4 main influential factors - eco-awareness, forgetfulness, convenience, and social norms to generate possible solutions.

As the result, over 90 ideas were generated. We grouped similar ideas together into 52 categories, and rated each category based on 13 criteria discussed in 6 main facets - feasibility, effectiveness, efficiency, novelty, team constraints, and impacts.

The last top five ideas were relevant to either gamification or social media, and they were more focusing on solving issues in eco-awareness, forgetfulness, and social norms. With these ideas, each of our team members used a storyboard to present our initial thoughts of a complete service by combining multiple ideas into one.

At this point, we were not sure which services regarding gamification or behavior tracking work better for users. Therefore, we decided to conduct the 2nd-round study to better understand users’ preferences and habits.

Supplemental User Studies

In order to have a larger sample size within a very limited time frame, we chose to use surveys.

We found that more than two thirds of students would use behavior tracking apps, but not so many of them played games. Also, the top main reasons behind doing an activity was being entertained, and students enjoyed doing with friends and families. Additionally, we validated that eco-friendly awareness was the most important factor on bring ones’ own mugs.

Motivations of doing an activity: being entertained > enjoying doing with friends/families > aligning with one's values.

Motivations of bringing one’s own mugs: eco-friendly awareness > financial incentives > social norms.

Most frequently used apps: social networking apps > search tools > productivity apps.

More than 2/3 people used an app to track behaviors (health, fitness, goals, productivity, etc.).

Only 1/4 people played games (mostly not).

Based on these findings, we developed our preliminary thoughts of the potential solution. It was a mobile app combining with a bluetooth-embedded smart sleeve with sensors which can detect and record mug usage.

Design Decisions

Through several rounds of discussions, we finally made some important decisions to ensure the effectiveness, efficiency, and engagement of our service.

Saving User Time

Automatic reminding and recording features are designed to save student time from checking and operating frequently.

Tracking & Sharing

To continuously motivate people to bring their own mugs and have positive influences, we took tracking usage records and sharing achievements as main features combining with visualizations to enhance engagement.

Sensors & Bluetooth

Deformation and gyroscope sensors were decided to be applied based on accuracy and cost concerns. A Bluetooth-embedded smart sleeve with the sensors embedded can detect the pressure from user grip, the orientation of the mug, and sync the usage data with an app.

Proximity & Time Detection

Reminders would be sent at key moments – in the morning of a day with free food events – for users to bring their mugs. Once the phone is close to the mug, and 20 minutes after the last clock alarm sets off, the reminder will be triggered.

Service Design & Prototypes

In the next phase, we clarified the work flow and how the service could be supported with a simple service blueprint. It started from users waking up in the morning and going to classes to tracking usage results and sharing with friends.

We also created low-fi and high-fi wireframes to present the key features including settings, reminders, tracking, and sharing.

We brought in entertainment factors by visualizing usage data with a tree mascot. The tree would grow up as users use their own mugs, which represented the idea of saving trees by reducing the usage of disposable cups.

Design Evaluation & Refinement

To ensure our solution could be accepted by target users and effectively solve problems, we evaluated the design by walking through the service with 5 students.

Below are four key changes we made based on user feedback:

Users expressed that too many notifications would be annoying, so we changed the design as the system will only send a reminder (only once) at the timing of 20 minutes after the last morning alarm set off (default settings) when the smart sleeve detects that the user’s phone exceeds the set distance. Both the distance and the time could be customized.

Based on user’s expectations, we added on real data with visualizations to present real user impact on the environment.

Users cared about their friends’ usage, so we added on a feature for users to view and compare achievements with their friends.

Users were desired to know the whole picture of the impact. They pointed out that the individual impact was small, but the impact of the whole community could be huge. Therefore, we added on the information on this new page to serve the need.

Reflection

Limitation

There was a lack of collecting information and feedback from relevant stakeholders at school.

Only issues at the individual level were addressed.

The design could not be applied to all types of users.

Next Step

Do more studies on habit or behavior change to help refine the solution.

Conduct more testings on the concept and the new features.

Adapt the service to a broader context such as office workers or people traveling to different meeting locations throughout the day.
